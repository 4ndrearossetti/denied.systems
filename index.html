the problem

Most small autonomous aircraft are built on a quiet assumption: that a constellation of satellites will always tell them where they are. In contested airspace that assumption fails first. Jamming removes the signal; spoofing replaces it with a lie, which is worse. An airframe that cannot answer where am I? without outside help is not autonomous — it is remote-controlled by whoever controls the spectrum.

This site maps the engineering that removes that assumption: how a vehicle keeps a usable estimate of its own position, velocity, and attitude when every external reference is degraded, denied, or hostile.

navigation without the constellation

The core discipline is GNSS-denied navigation: flying on what the airframe can sense for itself. Inertial sensors give you dead reckoning that drifts without bound; everything else in the stack exists to fight that drift — terrain and magnetic references, celestial fixes, signals of opportunity, and above all, cameras.

The workhorse of the camera-based approach is visual-inertial odometry: fusing high-rate IMU integration with visual feature tracks to hold drift to a fraction of a percent of distance travelled, on compute that fits a small airframe's power budget.

from estimate to action

A position estimate is only useful if the vehicle can act on it under the same constraints it was made under. Perception-to-control on embedded compute, guidance laws that tolerate noisy state, and mission logic that degrades gracefully instead of failing brittle — these are the downstream halves of the same problem, and later pages will treat them with the same field-guide discipline.
